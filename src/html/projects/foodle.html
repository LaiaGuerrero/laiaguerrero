FOODLE

An app which provides better solutions to never put food to waste again.

Overview

Imagine Foodle as your personal assistant for reducing food waste—guiding you through smart, practical ways to handle food at home. Using an intuitive decision tree, Foodle helps young people decide if sprouting potatoes are still safe to eat and discover suitable ways to use their leftovers.

My Role

Together with my team, I was involved in the entire product design process, from research and concept development to interface design and usability testing. Foodle was developed in collaboration with Lauria Kwan, Malena Guevgeozián and Anthony Oparah.

THE CHALLENGE

The initial challenge, presented during the Master’s in User Experience and Interface Design at ESDI Higher School of Design, was:

→ How might we make young people more aware of responsible food consumption?

This question served as the foundation for our project, inspiring us to design an engaging digital product that effectively addresses the issue of responsible food consumption and food waste.

UNDERSTAND

Desk Research

The issue of food waste is one of the most pressing challenges facing the world today, encompassing both social and environmental considerations. By throwing away food, we are also wasting the resources and energy used in its cultivation, harvesting, transportation, and packaging. The extent of this problem can be seen in the statistics from the World Wildlife Fund (WWF), which reports that food waste is responsible for 10% of total greenhouse gas emissions, a staggering amount that rivals the emissions produced by all cars in the US and Europe combined.

Only in Catalonia:

→ 62,000 tonnes of food is wasted annually, 7% of total food purchased.

→ 35 kg of food are wasted per inhabitant per year.

→ Households in Barcelona generate the most food waste in Catalonia, 58% of the total.


Target audience demographics

We began by identifying the ages that fall under the young people group. According to the United Nations, these are persons between the ages of 15 and 24 years. However, since our challenge relates to consumption, we expanded this by identifying what ages are classified as a young consumer - a person who purchases goods and services for personal use.

According to different sources (Leger, PICTET, EuroMonitor, etc.) young consumers as people under 40 years. We focused on Barcelona residents, as our team was based there during the research.

Exploring audience pain-points and behaviours

At the start of the project, we recognized our limited understanding of the challenges related to food consumption and waste. To bridge this gap, we conducted research to better understand the target audience’s lifestyle and their approach to food management. Research techniques used:

→ Informal interviews

We interviewed 12 volunteers under the age of 40 who live in Barcelona and claim to buy their own food.

→ Observation

Some interviews were conducted in settings like restaurants or while participants were cooking at home, allowing us to observe their habits related to food waste firsthand.

Affinity Diagram

The data collected was organized using an Affinity Diagram, which helped us identify patterns and common behaviors:

WASTE FOOD IS WASTE MONEY

  • I often forgot about the food I buy, and it just goes bad, which is frustating and expensive.
  • Every time I throw food away, I think about how much it cost me.
  • Wasting food feels like I'm not managing my budget well.

NO TIME

  • I rarely have time to plan meals or think about food waste.
  • Cooking feels like such a time-consuming task, so I often buy ready-made meals, even if some fresh ingredients go to waste.
  • I want to save food, but I’m very busy to find solutions.

NEED FOR EDUCATION

  • I know food waste is a problem, but I don’t know where to start learning.
  • ’ve never been taught how to store food properly or how to manage leftove
  • It would be helpful to have a guide tailored to beginners like me.

IMPULSE BUYING

  • Bulk buying seems economical, but it often leads to wasted food at my house.
  • Promotions at the supermarket tempt me to buy too much, and it ends up in the trash.
  • I tend to overestimate how much fresh produce I’ll need, but then they expire before I get to them.

LACK OF USEFUL SOLUTIONS

  • It’s hard to find tools that align with my busy schedule.
  • A lot of solutions I’ve seen are too complicated or not user-friendly.
  • Most of the apps to save food are overwhelming or not practical for my lifestyle.

HELPING OTHERS

  • It would be great if there was an easy way to connect with organizations that need food.
  • I always think someone else could use my leftovers, but I don’t know how to reach them quickly.
  • I feel guilty throwing away food, knowing someone else might be hungry.

Key insights

From the Affinity Diagram, three main insights emerged:

→ I can’t spend too much time taking action… it’s not my priority.

→ I don’t know how to not waste food I won’t consume in time.

→ I can't reach people who can benefit from the food I won’t consume.

We noticed a repeated and common problem among the participants: they struggle to avoid wasting food they cannot consume in time. This led us to consider it as a key problem to address through this project.

Deepening the understanding

To validate our findings and have a better understanding of the problem at hand, we conducted a second round of interviews (with the same 12 participants) and focusing on the following areas:

The knowledge about food waste prevention

  • We discovered that most people are unsure how to maximize the use of the food they have at home and how to avoid wasting them.

The type of information they need

  • They require simple, accessible information, as busy schedules often prevent them from adjusting their habits.

If education is really the right way to go

  • Young individuals lack the knowledge to prevent food waste; while some are motivated to address this issue, they do not know where to start.
  • So education is needed.

The pain-points associated with food management

  • Purchasing food in bulk due to economic advantages but failing to consume it all.
  • Difficulty understanding food expiration labels.
  • Limited awareness of proper storage methods to extend food freshness.

With all this information, are now prepared to redefine the problem scope.

DEFINE

Redefining the problem scope

To let us effectively address this issue, we have redefined our problem scope:

→ How might we busy young professionals with adaptable information to prevent food waste?

User persona

Using the qualitative data from the interviews, we defined the user profile to visualise various aspects of their behaviours and motivations.

User stories

As we narrowed down our focus, we drafted 3 point-of-view statements that helped us generate more clarity and describe who we are designing for specifically.

IDEATE

Generating initial solutions

We applied various ideation techniques to design an appropriate solution to the problem. After conducting a benchmark, we noticed that many of our ideas from the first brainstorming sessions were already launched in the apps market. To address this, we reevaluated our research data, explored new concepts, and combined ideas from different brainstorming sessions. This process led us to two main concepts:

→ CONCEPT 1: A portal that has access to possible solutions in preventing harmful food waste.

→ CONCEPT 2: A community platform facilitating easy and convenient access to food banks or solidarity fridges in the area.

We chose to move forward with Concept 1, as it aligned better with our goal. However, we decided not to proceed with this approach because it risked becoming a simple information dump, which would fail to offer an engaging and actionable experience for users.

At this point, our goal became clear:

→ To create a product that is not only informative but also actionable, interactive, and capable of empowering users to take meaningful steps in reducing food waste.

Getting to the final solution

During the Understand phase, we discovered that Google is the go-to platform for users seeking information and solving everyday problems. To delve deeper, we conducted a usability test to observe how users interact with Google, focusing on their behaviors, priorities, and frustrations.

User feedback revealed that information on Google is often scattered, overly complex, and occasionally inaccurate. This insight led us to explore ways to simplify and enhance the process of accessing reliable information, especially for practical use in the kitchen, such as during meal preparation or organizing the pantry/fridge.

Given the context of use and the need to reach a wider audience, we chose to design a mobile app. Research indicated that the number of internet users relying on mobile devices has grown exponentially in recent years, far surpassing desktop usage.

Refining Foodle's MVP

Foodle designed to provide users with actionable, relevant information about their food choices. To shape the MVP (Minimum Viable Product), we conducted research to identify commonly consumed and wasted foods, leading us to prioritize fruits and vegetables.

Initially, we designed Foodle screens around the category of fruits and vegetables. However, recognizing this scope was too wide, we conducted a survey with 200 young professionals in Barcelona, aiming to pinpoint the most consumed and wasted items within our target audience.

Based on the survey results (see graphs below), potatoes emerged as one of the most purchased yet wasted ingredients. This insight guided us to narrow our designs, centering on this ingredient to create a user-centered solution (the MVP will include more fruits and vegetables).

We also explored the most effective and engaging ways to present information in the app. Feedback from survey participants revealed their preferred learning formats and content styles, with videos and illustrations being the most voted options. These insights shaped the app's user experience to better meet their expectations.

The core function of Foodle will enable consumers to explore different ways to make the most of their fruits and vegetables. fruits and vegetables. This feature incorporates the following key elements into the MVP, refined through multiple sprints with a low-fidelity prototype:

FEATURES FOR THE MVP

  • Food-based search: Enables consumers to view information about a specific fruit or vegetable.

  • Food's state selection: Provides appropriate information based on the quality of the food (e.g., fresh, slightly spoiled).
  • Recommended solutions selection: Allows users to choose a method they are interested in, such as recipes, storage tips, or preservation techniques.
  • Use of images, illustrations, and videos: To make information easier and faster to understand for users.
  • History section: Enables returning users to quickly access previously viewed items.
  • Donate map: Provides a map with the locations of organizations where users can donate their excess food.

PROTOTYPE

LEARNINGS

Responsible consumption is a huge topic to tackle, so we decided to focus on the issue of food waste to narrow down the area of investigation and seek viable solutions. The food waste issue is not a new topic of interest: many people have attempted to solve this problem, and numerous applications already exist but are not always effective.This made it more challenging for us, as we had to figure out a user-centered solution to address the issue in a way that brings value to the users.

The development of Foodle required extensive research and the application of design thinking techniques and a significant amount of time was spent on desk research, interviews, data analysis, and ideation. One of the misconceptions we had was the idea to improve the experience of food organization and planning from when users shop for groceries. This was a mistake because many of the ideas we generated already exist. When we assessed these ideas against the problems and needsof the users, we realized they didn’t directly solve the issues. As a result, we had to revisit our data and re-sort it.

We were reminded by our teachers not to fall in love with the ideas we create. It’s quite true as what I had observed with my team members. When we were so convinced with what we have formed and found out it’s not going to work, it disheartened us. So we knew we had to step up and put ourselves out to get to the solution concept we now have, that is, an app that uses a decision tree to provide the appropriate method to maximize food use.

Doing sprints was the key in making our design a success. By testing our design ideas as we developed them, it allowed us to discover the usability issues fast and continuously improve the interface and experience of our digital product. It was liberating to learn that in creating a user-centered design, nothing works perfectly for the first time and there will always be something to enhance as you test it with different users. Hence, it is crucial to identify the MVP of the product to concentrate on the features that appeal more to the user.

If I were to do this project again, I would form the interview questions differently as a lot of them seemed redundant and I would look deeper into the people’s habits, what they really waste and what causes it. I would also seek for an expert in the field (which we have failed to do) to learn the viable ways to handle food accurately, and what they think we should do to avoid food wasting.
