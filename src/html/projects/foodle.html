FOODLE

an app that helps you decide how to make the most of your food.

Overview

Imagine Foodle as your personal assistant for reducing food waste—guiding you through smart, practical ways to handle food at home. Using an intuitive decision tree, Foodle helps young people decide if sprouting potatoes are still safe to eat and discover suitable ways to use their leftovers.

My Role

I was involved in the entire product design process, from research and concept development to interface design and usability testing. Foodle was developed during my Master’s in User Experience and Interface Design and in collaboration with Lauria Kwan, Malena Guevgeozián and Anthony Oparah.

THE CHALLENGE

The initial challenge, presented during the Master’s in User Experience and Interface Design at ESDI Higher School of Design, was:

→ How might we make young Barcelona residents more aware of responsible food consumption?

This question served as the foundation for our project, inspiring us to design an engaging digital product that effectively addresses the issue of responsible food consumption and food waste.

UNDERSTAND

Desk Research

The issue of food waste is one of the most pressing challenges facing the world today, encompassing both social and environmental considerations. By throwing away food, we are also wasting the resources and energy used in its cultivation, harvesting, transportation, and packaging. The extent of this problem can be seen in the statistics from the World Wildlife Fund (WWF), which reports that food waste is responsible for 10% of total greenhouse gas emissions, a staggering amount that rivals the emissions produced by all cars in the US and Europe combined.

Only in Catalonia:

  • 62,000 tonnes of food is wasted annually, 7% of total food purchased.
  • 35 kg of food are wasted per inhabitant per year.
  • Households in Barcelona generate the most food waste in Catalonia, 58% of the total.

Target audience demographics

We began by identifying the ages that fall under the young people group. According to the United Nations, these are persons between the ages of 15 and 24 years. However, since our challenge relates to consumption, we expanded this by identifying what ages are classified as a young consumer - a person who purchases goods and services for personal use.

According to different sources (Leger, PICTET, EuroMonitor, etc.) young consumers as people under 40 years.

Exploring audience pain-points and behaviours

At the start of the project, we recognized our limited understanding of the challenges related to food consumption and waste. To bridge this gap, we conducted research to better understand the target audience’s lifestyle and their approach to food management. We used the following research techniques:

  • Informal interviews: We interviewed 12 volunteers under the age of 40 who live in Barcelona and claim to buy their own food.
  • Observation: Some interviews were conducted in settings like restaurants or while participants were cooking at home, allowing us to observe their habits related to food waste firsthand.

During research, we focused in the following areas:

→ Knowledge about food waste

→ Pain-points associated to food management

→ Education is the right way to go?

→ Type of information needed

Affinity Diagram

The data collected was organized using an Affinity Diagram, which helped us identify patterns and common behaviors:

WASTE FOOD IS WASTE MONEY

  • I often forgot about the food I buy, and it just goes bad, which is frustating and expensive.
  • Every time I throw food away, I think about how much it cost me.
  • Wasting food feels like I'm not managing my budget well.

NO TIME

  • I rarely have time to plan meals or think about food waste.
  • Cooking feels like such a time-consuming task, so I often buy ready-made meals, even if some fresh ingredients go to waste.
  • I want to save food, but I’m very busy to find solutions.

IMPULSE BUYING

  • Bulk buying seems economical, but it often leads to wasted food at my house.
  • Promotions at the supermarket tempt me to buy too much, and it ends up in the trash.
  • I tend to overestimate how much fresh produce I’ll need, but then they expire before I get to them.

NEED FOR EDUCATION

  • I know food waste is a problem, but I don’t know where to start learning.
  • I’ve never been taught how to store food properly or how to manage leftove
  • It would be helpful to have a guide tailored to beginners like me.

LACK OF USEFUL SOLUTIONS

  • It’s hard to find tools that align with my busy schedule.
  • A lot of solutions I’ve seen are too complicated or not user-friendly.
  • Most of the apps to save food are overwhelming or not practical for my lifestyle.

HELPING OTHERS

  • It would be great if there was an easy way to connect with organizations that need food.
  • I always think someone else could use my leftovers, but I don’t know how to reach them quickly.
  • I feel guilty throwing away food, knowing someone else might be hungry.

DEFINE

Key insights

From the Affinity Diagram, three main insights emerged:

→ I can’t spend too much time taking action… it’s not my priority.

→ I don’t know how to not waste food I won’t consume in time.

→ I can't reach people who can benefit from the food I won’t consume.

We noticed a repeated and common problem among the participants: they struggle to avoid wasting food hey cannot consume in time. This led us to consider it as a key problem to address through this project.

With all this information, are now prepared to redefine the problem scope.

Redefining the problem scope

To let us effectively address this issue, we have redefined our problem scope:

→ How might we busy young professionals with adaptable information to prevent food waste?

User stories

As we narrowed down our focus, we drafted 3 point-of-view statements that helped us generate more clarity and describe who we are designing for specifically.

Marc, with plenty of oranges in his pantry, needs to know how to avoid food wasting, as buying in bulk is cheaper but too much for his family.

Marc, preparing a meal with potatoes, needs to know if his ingredients are safe to eat since they are starting to sprout.

Marc, who found soft carrots and overripe bananas in the fridge, needs to know how to keep food fresh longer and prevent it from spoiling so quickly.

IDEATE

Generating initial solutions

We applied various ideation techniques to design an appropriate solution to the problem. After conducting a benchmark, we noticed that many of our ideas from the first brainstorming sessions were already launched in the apps market. To address this, we reevaluated our research data, explored new concepts, and combined ideas from different brainstorming sessions. This process led us to two main concepts:

  • CONCEPT 1: A portal that has access to possible solutions in preventing harmful food waste.

  • CONCEPT 2: A community platform facilitating easy and convenient access to food banks or solidarity fridges in the area.

We chose to move forward with Concept 1, as it aligned better with our goal. However, we decided not to proceed with this approach because it risked becoming a simple information dump, which would fail to offer an engaging and actionable experience for users.

At this point, our goal became clear:

→ To create a product that is not only informative but also actionable, interactive, and capable of empowering users to take meaningful steps in reducing food waste.

Getting to the final solution

During the Understand phase, we discovered that Google is the go-to platform for users seeking information and solving everyday problems. To delve deeper, we conducted a usability test to observe how users interact with Google, focusing on their behaviors, priorities, and frustrations.

User feedback revealed that information on Google is often scattered, overly complex, and occasionally inaccurate. This insight led us to explore ways to simplify and enhance the process of accessing reliable information, especially for practical use in the kitchen, such as during meal preparation or organizing the pantry/fridge.

Given the context of use and the need to reach a wider audience, we chose to design a mobile app since research indicates that the number of internet users relying on mobile devices has grown exponentially in recent years, far surpassing desktop usage.

Here is Foodle: our final solution

Refining Foodle's MVP

Foodle is designed to provide users with actionable, relevant information about their food choices. To shape the MVP (Minimum Viable Product), we conducted research to identify commonly consumed and wasted foods, leading us to prioritize fruits and vegetables.

Initially, we designed Foodle screens around the category of fruits and vegetables. However, recognizing this scope was too wide, we conducted a survey with 200 young professionals in Barcelona, aiming to pinpoint the most consumed and wasted items within our target audience.

Based on the survey results, potatoes emerged as one of the most purchased yet wasted ingredients. This insight guided us to narrow our designs, centering on this ingredient to create a user-centered MVP solution.

We also explored the most effective and engaging ways to present information in the app. Feedback from survey participants revealed their preferred learning formats and content styles, with videos and illustrations being the most voted options.

Foodle's MVP

The core function of Foodle will enable consumers to explore different ways to make the most of their fruits and vegetables. This feature incorporates the following key elements into the MVP, refined through multiple sprints with a low-fidelity prototype:

FOOD-BASED SEARCH

  • Enables consumers to view information about a specific fruit or vegetable.

FOOD'S STATE SELECTION

  • Provides appropriate information based on the quality of the food (e.g., fresh, slightly spoiled, already spoiled).

RECOMMENDED SOLUTIONS

  • Allows users to choose a method they are interested in: recipes, storage tips, or preservation techniques.

USE OF GRAPHICS

  • Images, illustrations, and videos to make information easier and faster to understand for users.

HISTORY SECTION

  • Enables returning users to quickly access previously viewed items.

DONATE MAP

  • Provides a map with the locations of organizations where users can donate their excess food.

LEARNINGS & IMPACT

Responsible consumption and food waste are ongoing concerns. Many have attempted to address these issues, and numerous applications exist, yet they often fall short. This made our challenge even greater: we had to design a user-centered solution that truly added value.

Developing Foodle required extensive research and the application of design thinking methodologies. We invested significant time in desk research, interviews, data analysis, and ideation. Initially, we focused on improving food organization and planning from the moment users shop for groceries. However, we soon realized this approach had already been explored and did not directly address the core problem.

One of the most valuable lessons was learning not to get attached to our initial ideas. When we discovered that some of our assumptions didn’t work, it was discouraging. However, pushing through this setback led us to a more effective solution: an app that uses a decision tree to recommend the best method for maximizing food use.

Another key takeaway was the importance of iterative testing. Running sprints allowed us to quickly identify usability issues and continuously refine the interface and user experience. Defining the MVP early on helped us focus on the features that were most valuable to users.

If I were to redo this project, I would refine our interview questions to avoid redundancy and explore users’ habits more deeply—what they waste and why. Additionally, consulting an expert in the field would have provided valuable insights into practical solutions for reducing food waste.

Foodle was our final Master’s project for the Master’s in User Experience and Interface Design at ESDI Higher School of Design, and the expert panel awarded it the highest distinction: a 10/10 grade.
